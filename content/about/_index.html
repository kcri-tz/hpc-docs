History

The KCRI HPC started out in 2014 as three Dell Precision 7400 laptop workstations: genie, crick and watson. Each sported a dual quad core i7-3740QM CPU, 32GB memory and a 1TB HDD. Genie and crick are still in service today as entry nodes to the cluster. Watson regrettably processed its final bits in 2019, but is still with us for its spares.

The laptop trio came with our first whole genome sequencing project, appropriately called "The WGS Project". The WGS Project was a collaboration – the first of many – with Frank Aarestrup's genomic epidemiology group at DTU and brought us our first sequencer: the MiSeq. This marked the start of KCRI's Genomics & Bioinformatics unit.

Together with CGE's bioinformaticians, Zwets ported the well-known online CGE services to the laptops, solving the then still very prominent problem of doing large data transfers over flimsy internet connections: move the computation to Africa. We later integrated the services into the "BAP", our standard pipeline for bacterial sequence analysis. The BAP is open source software, available at https://github.com/kcri-tz/kcri-cge-bap.

The "3-laptop HPC" performed many hundreds of analyses of hospital isolates, feeding into the PhD dissertations of Tolbert Sonda and Happiness Kumburu, who now head KCRI's Genomics & Bioinformatics unit, with Marco van Zwetselaar (Zwets) as the bioinformatics wizard.

Our early set-up sufficed for most purposes, and we were grateful to have access to DTU's Computerome for the heavy lifting. However, as one of very few centres in Africa where both sequencing and bioinformatics were happening on-site, the unit had no trouble attracting new projects. Storage was rapidly becoming an issue, especially when we started doing Nanopore sequencing too. And clearly by 2018 that 32GB memory ceiling was seriously getting in the way.

This is when James Nokes's GeMVi project brought relief with a budget for our first "proper" bioinformatics server. Zwets spent a ridiculous amount of time on the ThinkMate site, configuring a server that would give the most bang for the buck: ample storage, lots of memory, and a powerful – while still affordable – GPU. The outcome was turing: 128 cpu, 3000 gpu, 2TB memory, 128TB storage. Yes, 2TB memory – that even beat the largest large-memory node in DTU Computerome 😎. And all this within a Â£30,000 budget.

Cool story: when turing had finally arrived, the first thing Zwets did now that he had a large memory machine, was analyse all bacterial sequences with GTDB-Tk. And sure enough, spotted a new species!

Features

The HPC now has two entry nodes (genie and crick), two compute nodes (turing, hopper) and a storage node (babbage). A node focused on public access and data sharing (shannon) will be added in 2022.
